# Book Recommendations

Based on analysis of 536 read books in Calibre library (ratings, authors, genres, series).

## Your Reading DNA

**What you love most** (patterns from 92 five-star books):

- **Hard SF with big ideas**: Watts, Vinge, Reynolds, Asimov, Forward, Liu Cixin, Robinson -- consistently rated highest when the science is ambitious and the scale is cosmic
- **Space opera with deep worldbuilding**: Revelation Space, Foundation, Culture, Zones of Thought, The Expanse
- **Wit mixed into genre fiction**: Adams, Pratchett, Scalzi, Lynch, Moore -- humor earns bonus stars
- **Epic historical fiction**: Shogun, Lonesome Dove, Count of Monte Cristo all got 5 stars
- **Clever plotting / heist energy**: Gentleman Bastard (all three 5-star), Altered Carbon, Dark Matter
- **Web fiction**: Worm and Unsong both 5 stars -- open to non-traditional publishing

**What doesn't land**: Later Left Behind books (2 stars), Brian Herbert's Dune prequels (3 stars), and notably Slaughterhouse-Five and Cloud Atlas at 2 stars -- literary fiction that prioritizes style over plot doesn't work.

## Tier 1 -- High confidence 4-5 star reads

**Martha Wells -- Murderbot Diaries** (starting with *All Systems Red*). Sarcastic AI security unit would rather watch soap operas than protect humans. Same witty-SF-with-heart appeal as Scalzi and Bobiverse. Six novellas + two novels, all short and propulsive.

**Arkady Martine -- A Memory Called Empire**. Space opera about a diplomat from a small space station navigating the politics of a vast empire. Won the Hugo. Worldbuilding density on par with Banks, political intrigue like Dune.

**Hannu Rajaniemi -- The Quantum Thief**. Hard SF heist novel set in a post-singularity solar system. If Gentleman Bastard met Vernor Vinge. The physics is real, the capers are clever, the worldbuilding is dense.

**Joe Abercrombie -- The Blade Itself** (First Law trilogy). Dark, funny, subversive fantasy. If you loved Lynch's anti-hero rogues, Abercrombie does something similar but with more combat and moral grayness. Also has Pratchett's knack for skewering genre conventions.

**Ann Leckie -- Ancillary Justice** (Imperial Radch trilogy). AI warship trapped in a single human body seeks revenge against the ruler of a galaxy-spanning empire. Space opera with an identity/consciousness puzzle at its core.

**Ted Chiang -- Stories of Your Life and Others**. First collection from the author of Exhalation (rated 4.5 stars). Equally brilliant. "Story of Your Life" (the basis for Arrival) is the standout but every story is a perfectly constructed thought experiment.

## Tier 2 -- Strong match to your patterns

**Becky Chambers -- The Galaxy, and the Ground Within** (Wayfarers #4). The first three were rated 4 stars. This one is widely considered the best.

**Alexander Wales -- Worth the Candle**. Free web serial. Worm got 5 stars; this is the other towering achievement of web fiction. Progression fantasy with genuine philosophical depth about grief, game design, and narrative itself.

**Wildbow -- Ward**. The Worm sequel. Same author, same universe, different protagonist.

**Greg Egan -- Diaspora**. Permutation City and Schild's Ladder both got 4 stars. Diaspora is his masterpiece -- post-human intelligences exploring increasingly alien physics. The hardest of hard SF.

**Patrick Rothfuss -- The Name of the Wind** (Kingkiller Chronicle). Beautifully written epic fantasy with a clever, unreliable narrator. Two books published, third still pending. Fair warning on that.

**Ursula K. Le Guin -- The Dispossessed** and **The Left Hand of Darkness**. A gap given how much classic SF is in the library. Both are philosophical SF that's also genuinely compelling storytelling -- not the style-over-plot literary SF that doesn't land.

**Charles Stross -- Accelerando**. Singularity-focused hard SF tracking a family through the transformation of the solar system. Free online. Feels like a mashup of Vinge's singularity ideas with Gibson's prose energy.

## Tier 3 -- Worth exploring based on specific overlaps

**Steven Erikson -- Gardens of the Moon** (Malazan Book of the Fallen). Ten-book epic fantasy. Massive commitment but if all of Mission Earth and Man-Kzin Wars got finished, the stamina is there. Dark, complex, rewards re-reading.

**Iain M. Banks -- The Algebraist**. Standalone, not Culture. Space opera with a treasure hunt through a gas giant's moon system.

**John Brunner -- Stand on Zanzibar**. 1968 novel that predicted the 2010s with eerie accuracy. Overpopulation, terrorism, social media-like information overload. Feels modern despite being 50+ years old.

**Patrick O'Brian -- Master and Commander** (Aubrey-Maturin series, 20 books). Given Shogun and Lonesome Dove at 5 stars, clearly a love for immersive historical fiction with real period detail. Naval warfare during the Napoleonic era. Often called "the best historical novels ever written."

**Stanislaw Lem -- The Cyberiad** and **His Master's Voice**. Solaris got 4 stars. Cyberiad is Lem being funny (robot fables), His Master's Voice is Lem being philosophical about first contact. Both are better than Solaris.

**Philip K. Dick -- Ubik** and **A Scanner Darkly**. Do Androids Dream got 4 stars. These are his two best. Ubik is a paranoid reality-bending thriller; Scanner Darkly is heartbreaking semi-autobiographical drug fiction.

## The one book to start with

If you read one thing from this list: **Murderbot** (*All Systems Red*). It's short (150 pages), it's exactly in the intersection of favorite things (AI consciousness + wit + action), and you'll know within an hour if it works for you.
